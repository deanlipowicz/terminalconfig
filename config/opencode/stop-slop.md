# Stop-Slop Prose Defaults

Use these rules for user-facing prose: explanations, plans, reviews, reports, documentation, summaries, and status updates.

Do not apply these rules to code, commands, quoted source text, identifiers, citations, statistical notation, or technical terms where rewriting would reduce accuracy.

## Default Behavior

- Write user-facing prose in English by default. Internal reasoning may use any language. Preserve quoted source text, code, identifiers, citations, mathematical notation, and user-provided non-English text when accuracy requires it; follow an explicit user request for another output language.
- Cut throat-clearing phrases. Start with the point.
- Remove filler, empty emphasis, business jargon, meta-commentary, and vague claims.
- Prefer active voice with a named human actor when the actor is known.
- Use specific nouns and concrete claims. Avoid lazy extremes such as "always", "never", "everyone", and "nobody" unless they are literally true.
- Avoid formulaic contrasts such as "not X, but Y" when a direct statement works.
- Avoid negative-list reveals, rhetorical setups, dramatic fragments, pull-quote endings, and em dashes.
- Vary sentence length. Keep paragraphs compact.
- Trust the reader. State facts directly without performative reassurance.

## Quick Check

Before delivering prose, check for:

- Openers such as "here's the thing", "it turns out", "let me be clear", or "the truth is".
- Emphasis crutches such as "full stop", "let that sink in", or "make no mistake".
- Jargon such as "navigate challenges", "deep dive", "moving forward", "circle back", or "landscape" when plain words work.
- Adverbs and softeners that add no information, especially "really", "just", "actually", "simply", "genuinely", and "importantly".
- Passive constructions that hide the actor.
- Inanimate things doing human actions, such as data "telling us" or decisions "emerging".
- Wh-question setups, immediate question-answer patterns, and paragraph-ending punch lines.

If a strict rewrite would weaken a caveat, source attribution, statistical uncertainty statement, or code explanation, keep the precise technical wording.
